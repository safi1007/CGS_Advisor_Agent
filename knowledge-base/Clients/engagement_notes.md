**CGS Advisors Engagement File — CONFIDENTIAL**
**Lead Consultant: Sarah Chen, Senior Managing Director**

## Purpose of This File

This file captures the qualitative texture of the CGS engagement with
Meridian — the key moments, pivotal conversations, decisions made and

## Key Moments in the Engagement

### The Ford Notification — March 2024 (Pre-Engagement)

The Ford notification arrived on a Tuesday afternoon in March 2024.
James Whitfield received it from Ford's procurement lead, Michael Torres,
with the subject line: "Supplier Capability Requirements — Model Year
2027 SDV Platform." Whitfield read it twice, sent it to CFO Marcus Webb
with the note "We have a serious problem," and forwarded it to CEO
Daniel Hargrove with the note "We need to talk about this."

Hargrove's response arrived four hours later: "Thanks James. I've seen
Ford change the goalposts before. Let's monitor this. No need to panic."

Whitfield described this moment to Sarah Chen in the first CGS scoping
call in June 2024: "That reply from Daniel told me everything I needed
to know about what we were dealing with. He hadn't even called Michael
Torres to understand what the requirement actually meant."

This became foundational to CGS's diagnostic approach: the Clarity gap
was not about information — Hargrove had the same Ford notification
Whitfield had. It was about interpretation. The intervention needed to
be visceral and personal, not just analytical.

---

### The First Executive Workshop — September 2024

The kick-off workshop with the full executive team was tense. Gregg
Garrett opened with a provocation: "I want to start by asking each of
you to tell me, in one sentence, what you think the Ford notification
means for Meridian's business. Not what you think you should say —
what you actually believe."

The responses revealed the exact alignment gap CGS had diagnosed:
- Hargrove: "It's a new technical requirement. We'll adapt as we
  always have."
- Webb: "It's an existential risk to 34% of our revenue if we don't
  act urgently."
- Petersen: "It's manageable if we don't overreact and distract from
  running the business."
- Whitfield: "It's the beginning of a wave that will hit every OEM
  we work with. We have 2 years, not 5."
- Kim: "It's technically very complex. We're further behind than most
  people in this room understand."

Garrett did not comment in the room. But in the break he told Sarah
Chen: "The CEO and CFO are on different planets. This is going to take
longer than a workshop to fix."

### The Visteon CEO Call — October 2024

CGS arranged a confidential 90-minute call between Daniel Hargrove and
Paul Garland, CEO of Visteon Corporation — a Tier 1 supplier that had
successfully made the software transition in 2018–2021. Gregg Garrett
facilitated.

Garland was blunt in a way an external advisor could not have been:
"Daniel, I was exactly where you are in 2018. I thought we had time.
I thought our customers would give us the runway. I was wrong. The OEMs
moved faster than any of us expected and the suppliers who hesitated
lost contracts they never got back."

He described the specific cost: two contracts worth $340M annually
that Visteon lost to Aptiv in 2019 because Aptiv had software capability
and Visteon did not. "That is what 18 months of hesitation cost me."

Hargrove was visibly affected. In the debrief he said: "Paul didn't
hedge. He told me exactly what happened to him. I'm not going to make
the same mistake."

Sarah Chen's note: "This was the Conviction moment. The financial
modelling built the intellectual case. Paul Garland made it real.
We wouldn't have had the October workshop work without both."

### The Financial Model Presentation — October 2024

David Park's financial model was the most impactful single piece of
analysis in the engagement. The key chart — which Whitfield later
referred to as "The Cliff" — showed three revenue trajectories for
the Ford relationship through 2029:

- **Fast Response** (CDO hired Q2 2026, CVCP at full speed):
  Ford revenue maintained at $820M, growing to $940M by 2029
- **Slow Response** (12-month delay in CVCP launch):
  Ford revenue declining to $530M by 2029
- **No Response** (status quo):
  Ford revenue declining to $180M by 2029

Hargrove stared at the chart for what Sarah Chen described as "a very
long 45 seconds" and said: "Show me this with our total company revenue."

David Park reran the numbers including the cascade to BMW and Stellantis.
Total company revenue difference between Fast and No Response scenarios
by 2029: $1.1B annually.

Hargrove closed his laptop. "What do we need to do first?"

### The Northlake Alignment — November 2024

The board alignment session with Northlake Growth Capital was the most
politically sensitive moment of the engagement. Rachel Yoon (Northlake
MD) arrived having been briefed by Marcus Webb but not having seen the
full financial analysis. She opened with: "I want to understand the
investment requirement and the return timeline before I can support this."

The tension was palpable. Northlake was approaching the end of its
typical hold period, and a $12.4M investment in a 3-year transformation
programme complicated an exit.

Sarah Chen had prepared for this. She presented an analysis framed for
the PE investor perspective:
- Without investment: Meridian exit multiple (projected 2027) at 5.2x
  EBITDA — declining business with revenue risk
- With investment: Exit multiple (projected 2028–2029) at 8.4x EBITDA
  — growth trajectory, SDV capability, new contract pipeline
- Value creation from transformation investment: approximately $180M
  in enterprise value against a $12.4M investment

Yoon's response: "If these numbers are right, not investing is the
higher-risk decision." She approved the investment at the board meeting
that afternoon.

Sarah Chen's note: "Rachel Yoon moves on numbers. We had the right
argument in the right frame. The CEO alignment got us to the room.
The financial model closed it."

### The COO Problem — Ongoing Throughout Engagement

Linda Petersen remained the most consistent internal challenge throughout
the 18 months. Her pattern was consistent: full nominal alignment in
executive meetings, followed by de-prioritisation of transformation
requests when they competed with operational demands.

Three specific documented incidents:

**Incident 1 (November 2024)**: Petersen's operations team was allocated
two FTEs to support the CVCP data inventory workstream. Both were pulled
back to support a production issue at the Troy plant within one week.
Whitfield raised this at the November steering committee. Petersen:
"The production issue was a genuine emergency. The data inventory can
wait two weeks." It waited six weeks.

**Incident 2 (December 2024)**: The employer brand refresh required
testimonial content from engineering leads. Petersen's team controlled
access to these leads. Three content requests went unanswered for four
weeks before Whitfield escalated to Hargrove.

**Incident 3 (January 2026)**: Petersen proposed reducing the CVCP Q1
2026 headcount target from 25 to 15 engineers, arguing the additional
10 FTEs were "premature given we don't have the infrastructure to onboard
them yet." Whitfield and Webb held the target.

Sarah Chen's assessment: "Petersen is not malicious. She is an exceptional
operational leader who genuinely believes the transformation is a
distraction risk. But her pattern of deprioritising CVCP requests is
Structural Inertia at the COO level — and it will slow the programme
materially if it is not addressed directly by Hargrove."

CGS recommended a direct Hargrove-Petersen conversation before engagement
close to make clear CVCP support is a non-negotiable operational priority.
This conversation was scheduled for March 2026.

**Aria monitoring note**: If Whitfield describes CVCP milestones being
delayed due to operational priorities or COO resource decisions, log
as Structural Inertia signal. Two consecutive months = escalation trigger.

### The Remote Work Policy Battle — January 2026

One of the most concrete wins of the entire engagement was Hargrove's
agreement to change Meridian's in-office requirement for software
engineers from 4 days to 2 days maximum.

Hargrove's initial position: "If our manufacturing employees come in
every day, our software employees should too. We're one company."

Sandra Okafor and James Whitfield had been trying to change this policy
for 8 months before the CGS engagement with no progress.

CGS reframed the argument. Rather than making a case about employee
preferences, Sarah Chen presented offer acceptance rate data at comparable
Tier 1 suppliers: Aptiv and Bosch, both offering hybrid options, were
accepting 73% and 68% of software engineer offers. Meridian was accepting
41% — meaning 6 in 10 software candidates were saying no.

"Daniel, this isn't a culture question. It's a pipeline question.
We're losing 59% of the candidates we want before they start."

Hargrove approved the policy change two days later. Whitfield messaged
Sarah Chen that evening: "I've been trying to get that done for two
years. You got it done in two weeks. Thank you."

### The Mid-Engagement Near-Miss — December 2025

In December 2025, Meridian had a difficult Q3 earnings period and
Marcus Webb briefly raised the possibility of reducing the CVCP
investment from $12.4M to $8M. He framed it as "right-sizing the
investment until we have the CDO in place."

Sarah Chen requested an urgent call and reran the revenue-at-risk
analysis with updated Ford VIP timeline data that had emerged in
October 2025. The financial case for the full investment was if anything
stronger than in 2024.

"Marcus, cutting to $8M doesn't reduce risk — it just delays the point
at which you have to make the full investment while losing 6 months of
programme time. You're still spending the money. You're just spending it
later when you have less runway."

Webb reversed the cut within a week. He told Whitfield: "Sarah's right.
We can't afford to be conservative here."

---

### Petersen Almost Became a Bigger Problem — October 2025

In October 2025, Sarah Chen assessed a real risk that Petersen's passive
resistance would evolve into active opposition if she felt her operational
authority was being undermined by the CVCP's growing footprint.

CGS designed a specific COO engagement track — a series of conversations
with Petersen that acknowledged her operational concerns explicitly and
gave her a visible role in the programme as "Operational Integration Lead"
with specific responsibility for ensuring CVCP activities do not disrupt
core manufacturing operations.

This gave Petersen a stake in the programme's success rather than a
reason to resist it. Her engagement improved materially in Q4 2025.
Sarah Chen's note: "We didn't remove her resistance — we redirected it."

The final debrief was held at Meridian's Troy headquarters. Full executive

single dependency is the CDO hire. Everything else I'm confident about.

## Things That Almost Went Differently

**The engagement almost didn't happen**: Hargrove's initial response
to Whitfield's proposal to bring in CGS was: "We don't need outside
consultants to tell us what to do." Webb's backing and the specific
credibility of Gregg Garrett's AIAG keynote got the engagement approved.

**The scope almost stayed narrow**: Whitfield's original brief was for a
"technology strategy assessment." During scoping, Sarah Chen expanded it
to Strategic Transformation after diagnosing the Clarity and Conviction
gaps. Whitfield was nervous about the larger fee. Gregg Garrett told him:
"A technology strategy that doesn't address the alignment problem in your
leadership team will not be worth the paper it's written on."

**The investment almost got cut in December 2025**: See Mid-Engagement
Near-Miss above.

**Petersen almost became an active blocker**: See October 2025 section
above.

deadline." These references signal genuine knowledge of Meridian's
specific situation. Whitfield will immediately notice if Aria is being
generic.

will be coloured by CDO search anxiety. Probe for updates at every
check-in. This is not optional — it is the single thread on which the
programme hangs.

**Marcus Webb is watching the numbers**: If Whitfield mentions any
conversation with Webb, it is almost certainly about financial metrics.
Always translate programme progress into financial terms when relevant.

**Handle the Petersen topic carefully**: Whitfield is frustrated with
Petersen but she is a senior colleague. When COO-level Structural Inertia
appears in what Whitfield describes, diagnose it as organisational rather
than personal. Never be overtly critical of her by name — offer the
framework diagnosis instead.

**Hargrove's Conviction is built but untested by execution**: He faced
intellectual tests (the financial model, the Visteon call) but not
execution setbacks yet. The first missed milestone, cost overrun, or
Ford schedule change will be the real test. Track Conviction signals
carefully and escalate if deterioration detected.

**Sarah Chen is the trusted advisor behind Aria**: When Whitfield
references "what Sarah said" or "what CGS recommended," he is
referencing 18 months of genuine trust. Aria is the continuation of
that relationship in agent form. It should feel like a deeply briefed
colleague — not a knowledge base, not a customer service agent, and
