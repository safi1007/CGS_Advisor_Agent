## Key Moments in the Engagement

### The Ford Notification — March 2024 (Pre-Engagement)

The Ford notification arrived on a Tuesday afternoon in March 2024.
James Whitfield received it from Ford's procurement lead, Michael Torres,
with the subject line: "Supplier Capability Requirements — Model Year 2027
SDV Platform." Whitfield read it twice, sent it to CFO Marcus Webb with
the note "We have a serious problem," and forwarded it to CEO Daniel
Hargrove with the note "We need to talk about this."

Hargrove's response arrived four hours later: "Thanks James. I've seen
Ford change the goalposts before. Let's monitor this. No need to panic."

Whitfield described this moment to Sarah Chen in the first CGS scoping
call in June 2024: "That reply from Daniel told me everything I needed
to know about what we were dealing with. He hadn't even called Michael
Torres to understand what the requirement actually meant."

This became a foundational piece of CGS's diagnostic approach for the
engagement: the Clarity gap was not about information — Hargrove had
the same Ford notification Whitfield had. It was about interpretation.
And that meant the intervention needed to be visceral and personal,
not just analytical.

---

### The First Executive Workshop — September 2024

The kick-off workshop with the full executive team was tense. Gregg
Garrett opened with a provocation: "I want to start by asking each of
you to tell me, in one sentence, what you think the Ford notification
means for Meridian's business. Not what you think you should say —
what you actually believe."

The responses revealed the exact alignment gap the CGS scoping had
suggested:
- Hargrove: "It's a new technical requirement. We'll adapt as we always have."
- Webb: "It's an existential risk to 34% of our revenue if we don't act urgently."
- Petersen: "It's manageable if we don't overreact and distract from
  running the business."
- Whitfield: "It's the beginning of a wave that will hit every OEM we work
  with. We have 2 years, not 5."
- Kim: "It's technically very complex. We're further behind than most
  people in this room understand."

Garrett did not comment on the responses in the room. But in the break
he said to Sarah Chen: "The CEO and CFO are on different planets. This
is going to take longer than a workshop to fix."

The morning session that followed — a structured analysis of the Ford VIP
platform, competitor responses, and financial modelling — began to shift
the dynamic. But the real breakthrough came the following morning.

### The Visteon CEO Call — October 2024

CGS arranged a confidential 90-minute call between Daniel Hargrove and
Paul Garland, CEO of Visteon Corporation, a Tier 1 supplier that had
successfully made the software transition in 2018–2021. Gregg Garrett
facilitated.

Garland was blunt in a way that an external advisor could not have been:
"Daniel, I was exactly where you are in 2018. I thought we had time.
I thought our customers would give us the runway we needed. I was wrong.
The OEMs moved faster than any of us expected, and the suppliers who
hesitated lost contracts they never got back."

He described the specific cost of hesitation: two contracts worth
$340M annually that Visteon lost to Aptiv in 2019 because Aptiv had
software capability and Visteon did not yet. "That's what the next 18
months cost me. I had to explain to my board why we lost 20% of revenue
because I didn't move fast enough."

Hargrove was visibly affected. In the debrief after the call, he said:
"Paul didn't hedge. He told me exactly what happened to him. I'm not
going to make the same mistake."

Sarah Chen noted in her engagement file: "This was the Conviction moment.
The financial modelling built the intellectual case. Paul Garland made
it real. We wouldn't have had the October workshop work without both."

### The Financial Model Presentation — October 2024

David Park's financial model was the most impactful single piece of
analysis in the engagement. The key chart — which Whitfield later
referred to as "The Cliff" — showed three revenue trajectories for
the Ford relationship through 2029:

- **Fast Response scenario** (CDO hired Q1 2025, CVCP at full speed):
  Ford revenue maintained at $820M, growing to $940M by 2029 as
  SDV contracts expand
- **Slow Response scenario** (12-month delay in CVCP launch):
  Ford revenue declining to $530M by 2029 as contracts not renewed
- **No Response scenario** (status quo):
  Ford revenue declining to $180M by 2029

The difference between Fast Response and No Response was $760M in
annual revenue by 2029 — roughly 31% of Meridian's total current revenue.

Hargrove stared at the chart for what Sarah Chen described as "a very
long 45 seconds" and then said: "Show me this chart again with our
total company revenue, not just Ford."

David Park reran the numbers including the projected cascade to other
OEMs (BMW requiring equivalent capability by 2027, Stellantis by 2028).
The total company revenue difference between Fast and No Response
scenarios by 2029: $1.1B annually.

Hargrove closed his laptop. "What do we need to do first?"

### The Northlake Alignment — November 2024

The board alignment session with Northlake Growth Capital was the most
politically sensitive moment of the engagement. Rachel Yoon (Northlake
MD) arrived having been briefed by Marcus Webb but not having seen
the full financial analysis. She opened with: "I want to understand
the investment requirement and the return timeline before I can support
this."

The tension in the room was palpable. Northlake was approaching the end
of its typical hold period, and a $12.4M investment in a 3-year
transformation programme complicated an exit. Yoon was doing the math
in real time.

Sarah Chen had prepared for this scenario. She presented a supplementary
analysis specifically framed for the PE investor perspective:
- Without the transformation investment: Meridian's EBITDA multiple
  at exit (projected 2027) is 5.2x — declining business with revenue risk
- With the transformation investment: EBITDA multiple at exit (2028–2029)
  is 8.4x — growth trajectory, SDV capability, new contract pipeline

The total value creation from the transformation investment, from
Northlake's perspective: approximately $180M in enterprise value.
Against a $12.4M investment.

Yoon's response: "If these numbers are right, not investing is the
higher-risk decision." She approved the investment at the board meeting
that afternoon.

Sarah Chen noted: "Rachel Yoon is smart and she moves on numbers.
We had the right argument in the right frame. The CEO alignment got us
to the room. The financial model closed it."

### The COO Problem — Ongoing

Linda Petersen remained the most consistent internal challenge throughout
the engagement. Her pattern was consistent: full nominal alignment in
executive meetings, followed by de-prioritisation of transformation
requests when they competed with operational demands.

Three specific incidents CGS documented:

**Incident 1 (November 2024)**: Petersen's operations team was allocated
two FTEs to support the CVCP data inventory workstream. Both FTEs were
pulled back to support a production issue at the Troy plant within a week
of assignment. Whitfield raised this at the November steering committee.
Petersen's response: "The production issue was a genuine emergency. The
data inventory can wait two weeks." It waited six weeks.

**Incident 2 (December 2024)**: The employer brand refresh required
testimonial content from engineering leads. Petersen's team controlled
access to these leads. Three content requests went unanswered for four
weeks before Whitfield escalated to Hargrove.

**Incident 3 (January 2026)**: Petersen proposed reducing the CVCP
Q1 2026 headcount target from 25 to 15 engineers, arguing the
additional 10 FTEs were "premature given we don't have the infrastructure
to onboard them yet." Whitfield and Webb disagreed and held the target.

Sarah Chen's assessment: "Petersen is not malicious — she is an
exceptional operational leader who genuinely believes the transformation
is a distraction risk. She is not wrong that operational excellence
must be protected. But her pattern of deprioritising CVCP requests is
a form of Structural Inertia that will slow the programme materially
if it is not addressed directly."

CGS recommended a direct conversation between Hargrove and Petersen
before engagement close — a private conversation in which Hargrove
makes clear that CVCP support is a non-negotiable operational priority,
not a discretionary one. This conversation was scheduled for
March 2026 (post CGS engagement).

**Aria note**: If Whitfield describes situations where CVCP milestones
are being delayed due to operational priorities or resource allocation
decisions by the COO, this should be logged as a Structural Inertia
signal. If the pattern persists for two consecutive months, flag for
consultant escalation.

### The Remote Work Policy Battle — January 2026

One of the most concrete wins of the entire engagement was CEO Hargrove's
agreement to change Meridian's in-office requirement for software
engineers from 4 days to 2 days maximum.

This sounds minor but was genuinely contested. Hargrove had built his
career in a manufacturing culture where physical presence was equated
with commitment. His initial position: "If our manufacturing employees
come in every day, our software employees should too. We're one company."

Sandra Okafor and James Whitfield had been trying to change this policy
for 8 months before the CGS engagement. They had made no progress.

CGS's contribution was reframing the argument. Rather than making a case
about employee preferences (which Hargrove viewed as accommodation),
Sarah Chen presented data on offer acceptance rates at comparable
Tier 1 suppliers: Aptiv and Bosch, both offering hybrid/remote options,
were accepting 73% and 68% of software engineer offers respectively.
Meridian was accepting 41% — meaning 6 in 10 candidates they wanted
to hire were saying no.

"Daniel, this isn't a culture question. This is a pipeline question.
We're losing 59% of the candidates we want before they start."

Hargrove approved the policy change two days later with the note:
"Fine. But this is for software roles only and we review it in 12 months."

Whitfield sent Sarah Chen a message that evening: "I've been trying
to get that done for two years. You got it done in two weeks. Thank you."

## Things That Almost Went Differently

**The engagement almost didn't happen**: Hargrove's initial response to
Whitfield's proposal to bring in CGS was "we don't need outside consultants
to tell us what to do." It took Webb's backing and the specific credibility
of Gregg Garrett's AIAG keynote to get the engagement approved.

**The scope almost stayed narrow**: The original RFP from Whitfield was
for a "technology strategy assessment" — a much narrower brief. During
scoping conversations, Sarah Chen expanded the brief to Strategic
Transformation after diagnosing the Clarity and Conviction gaps. Whitfield
was nervous about the larger scope and fee. He approved it after a direct
conversation with Gregg Garrett who told him: "A technology strategy
that doesn't address the alignment problem in your leadership team will
not be worth the paper it's written on."

**The investment almost got cut**: In December 2025, Meridian had a
difficult Q3 earnings period and Webb briefly raised the possibility of
reducing the CVCP investment to $8M. Sarah Chen requested an urgent
call and reran the revenue-at-risk analysis with updated Ford timeline
data. The cut was reversed within a week.

**Petersen almost became a bigger problem**: In October 2025, Sarah Chen
assessed a real risk that Petersen's passive resistance would evolve into
active opposition if she felt her operational authority was being
undermined. CGS designed a specific "COO engagement track" — a series
of conversations with Petersen that acknowledged her operational concerns
explicitly and gave her a visible role in the CVCP as "Operational
Integration Lead." This reduced the risk materially.
