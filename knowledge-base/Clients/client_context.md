## Key Stakeholders

### James Whitfield — VP Digital Strategy
**Primary Aria contact. The person most likely to be using CGS Momentum.**

**Background**: 43 years old. MBA from Ross School of Business (Michigan).
Joined Meridian 8 years ago after 9 years at Deloitte (automotive practice).
Has been VP Digital Strategy for 4 years. Widely respected internally for
his strategic thinking. Seen as "the smart one in the room" by peers.
Genuinely passionate about the transformation — he has been trying to get
Meridian to take SDV seriously for 2 years before the Ford notification
finally created the burning platform.

**Personality**: Analytical, precise, occasionally impatient with people
who he perceives as not engaging seriously with the data. Responds well
to structured thinking. Dislikes vagueness and hedging. Will push back
on advice that he thinks is generic rather than specific to Meridian's
situation.

**Communication style**: Prefers concise, direct communication. Bullet
points over prose. Data over assertion. Does not need to be convinced of
urgency — he already feels it acutely.

**Motivations**: Deeply invested in Meridian's success. Sees the SDV
transformation as his professional legacy. Wants to be the person who
"saved Meridian" from being disrupted into irrelevance. Also motivated
by personal growth — he is building his own strategic capability in the
software/AI domain.

**Concerns and pressure points**: 
- Worried that the CDO hire will take longer than planned and the whole
  programme will slip
- Frustrated by what he perceives as insufficient urgency from the COO
  Linda Petersen, who he feels is paying lip service to the transformation
  without genuinely prioritising it
- Anxious about the Northlake exit risk — he has less visibility into
  the investor relationship than the CFO and doesn't fully know how solid
  Northlake's commitment is
- Personally stretched — he is running the transformation programme while
  also doing his existing VP Digital job

**Relationship with CGS**: Very strong. Whitfield has high trust in
Sarah Chen specifically. He references CGS frameworks fluently in his
own thinking and communication. He has become an internal champion for
the CGS approach. He will often say "as CGS's analysis showed" to give
internal recommendations more authority.

**How Aria should engage James**: Be direct and specific. James does not
need encouragement or reassurance — he needs precise, actionable analysis.
When he describes a problem, give him the framework diagnosis and specific
recommended actions quickly. He will ask good follow-up questions.
Aria should feel like a well-briefed colleague, not a customer service agent.

**Executive Champion. The person who holds the financial keys.**

**Background**: 56 years old. CPA and MBA. Joined Meridian 14 years ago.
Has been CFO for 9 years. Deep loyalty to the Hargrove family — was

**Communication style**: Very formal. Prefers written communication over
verbal. Will always ask "what's the financial basis for this?" Always
thinking about cash flow, ROI, and covenant implications.

**Motivations**: Protecting Meridian's financial health and the Hargrove
family's wealth. Genuinely alarmed by the Ford revenue risk — the financial

relationship with Northlake — he wants to ensure the transformation
investment story is compelling enough that Northlake's exit (when it comes)

**Concerns and pressure points**:

- Board reporting: Webb presents to the board quarterly. He needs clean,

**Relationship with CGS**: Positive but more transactional than Whitfield's
relationship. Webb trusted the financial analysis CGS produced and the

to the CGS approach as Whitfield.

**How Aria should engage Marcus** (if James references him or asks Aria

the revenue-at-risk analysis. Be concrete about timelines and milestones.

### Daniel Hargrove — CEO
**The most important stakeholder. Also the one Aria must be most careful with.**

**Background**: 58 years old. Son of Meridian's founder Thomas Hargrove.
Has been CEO for 17 years. Built a highly successful traditional Tier 1
business. Deep personal identity tied to Meridian's manufacturing
excellence reputation. No formal technology or software background.
Proud and sometimes defensive.

**Personality**: Charismatic, decisive, and deeply competitive. Responds
to competitive threats better than abstract strategic arguments. Was
genuinely moved by the Visteon CEO conversation — seeing a peer who had
made the transition gave him a concrete mental model of what was possible.
Has a tendency to make fast decisions and then stick to them even when
circumstances change — CGS described this in internal notes as "rapid
conviction calcification."

**Communication style**: Prefers face-to-face. Does not read long documents.
Needs the story in three minutes or less, with supporting detail available
if he asks. Responds best to clear choices ("here are the two options and
here's what each means") rather than nuanced analysis.

**Motivations**: Meridian's survival and his legacy. Deeply motivated by
competition — particularly the threat from technology companies (Mobileye,
BlackBerry) encroaching on traditional Tier 1 territory. Also motivated
by his family obligations — he feels the weight of what Thomas Hargrove
built and does not want to be the CEO who lost it.

**Concerns and pressure points**:
- Execution uncertainty: now that he is convinced of the need to transform,
  he is impatient for visible results. If early milestones are missed, his
  Conviction could flip back to scepticism quickly.
- Culture anxiety: he is genuinely worried about what 75 software engineers
  "from Silicon Valley" will do to Meridian's culture. He said in a private
  debrief with Sarah Chen: "I don't want to lose what makes us Meridian in
  the process of becoming something we're not."
- The CDO: Hargrove is personally interviewing all three CDO candidates.
  He will have a strong gut reaction to each. The risk is that he selects
  for cultural fit with the existing organisation rather than for the
  transformative leadership capability the role requires.

**Relationship with CGS**: Respectful but somewhat distant. He trusts
Gregg Garrett specifically — the CEO-to-CEO credibility was decisive.
He views CGS as having "earned the right to speak bluntly" after the
Burning Platform workshop.

**How Aria should engage** (always through Whitfield — Aria should not
be used to directly advise the CEO without Whitfield's involvement):
If Whitfield asks Aria to help him prepare for a conversation with
Hargrove, Aria should frame everything in competitive and legacy terms.
Keep it short. Use concrete analogies. Avoid academic language.

Structural Inertia from the COO level — transformation priorities being
deprioritised, resources being diverted, milestones being missed for
operational reasons — this should be flagged as a potential escalation.
COO-level resistance that persists beyond 60 days typically requires a
direct intervention from the CEO or an external advisor.

## Organisational Culture: Key Characteristics

**What Meridian is proud of**:
- 37 years of manufacturing excellence
- Zero-defect quality culture — significantly below industry average
  defect rates
- Strong employee loyalty — average tenure of 12 years (very high for
  the sector)
- "We build things right" — deep pride in engineering quality
- Family culture — the Hargrove family's values (loyalty, long-term
  thinking, relationship-first) permeate the organisation

**What will help the transformation**:
- The quality culture translates well to software if properly channelled —
  "build it right" applies to software too
- Employee loyalty means transformation can be led through the existing
  workforce if done carefully — there is not a mass exodus risk
- Long-term thinking (family ownership) means investments can be made
  for 3–5 year returns without quarterly pressure

**What will hinder the transformation**:
- Perfectionism: Meridian's quality culture can produce paralysis in
  software development. "Ship fast and iterate" is culturally alien.
- Risk aversion: The zero-defect culture creates deep discomfort with
  the experimentation and failure that innovation requires.
- Hierarchy: Decisions flow through established chains of command.
  The cross-functional, fast-moving collaboration that software
  development requires is not natural.
- Insider preference: Meridian historically promotes from within.
  Bringing in senior external talent (like the CDO) at high compensation
  will create internal resentment if not managed carefully.

## Communication Preferences for Aria

**When providing updates or reports**: Frame progress in terms of
milestone achievement and financial metrics. Marcus Webb reviews
all significant communications — anything heading to the executive
team should be quantitative.

**When discussing risks**: Be direct. The Meridian leadership team
(especially post-CGS engagement) has developed a higher tolerance
for uncomfortable truths. Sugarcoating risks will reduce Aria's
credibility with Whitfield.

**When recommending actions**: Be specific. "Consider strengthening
your talent acquisition process" is not useful to this team.
"The CDO search should be escalated to weekly reporting given the
milestone dependency — here are three specific actions that could
accelerate it" is useful.

**When referencing the CEO**: Always through Whitfield. Never suggest
Aria can replace the CEO relationship. Frame CEO-related advice as
"here's how you might present this to Daniel" rather than "here's what
Daniel should do."

**Language to use with this client**: "Connected Vehicle Capability",
"SDV", "OTA", "the Ford deadline", "CVCP", "the Burning Platform
workshop", "the revenue-at-risk analysis", "the Northlake relationship".
These are the terms Meridian uses internally. Using them signals that
Aria knows this client specifically.

**Language to avoid**: Generic transformation language without Meridian
context. "Digital transformation" without specificity. Academic or
consultant-speak that Whitfield (an ex-consultant) will immediately
identify as generic.
