# The Three Cs Framework


## Overview

The Three Cs Framework is CGS Advisors' diagnostic lens for identifying why a client organisation is struggling to transform. In fifteen years of transformation advisory work across automotive, insurance, manufacturing, technology, and professional services, CGS has consistently found that transformation failure can be traced to a deficit in one or more of three dimensions:

**Clarity** — Does the leadership team have a shared, accurate, and compelling view of where the industry is going and what the organisation must become?

**Capability** — Does the organisation have the skills, processes, technology, and culture required to execute the transformation strategy?

**Conviction** — Does leadership have the courage, alignment, and sustained commitment to drive the transformation to completion despite uncertainty and resistance?

The Three Cs are not a sequence — they are simultaneous and interdependent. A deficiency in any one is sufficient to derail transformation regardless of how strong the other two are.

Every CGS engagement begins with a Three Cs diagnostic. The findings determine where CGS focuses its advisory effort.

## CLARITY

### Definition
Clarity is the degree to which an organisation's leadership team shares a common, accurate, and specific view of:
1. Where the industry is heading — the nature, speed, and implications of disruption
2. What the organisation must become — the strategy, business model, and capabilities required
3. Why this transformation is urgent — the competitive dynamics that make acting now better than waiting

Clarity is not simply about having information. Many leadership teams have access to the same industry research. The Clarity gap exists when leaders interpret that information differently, weight it differently, or fail to translate it into a shared sense of urgency and direction.

### What a Clarity Gap Looks Like

At the executive level:
- The CEO and CTO have fundamentally different views of how quickly the industry will change
- The board sees disruption as a 5–10 year horizon; management sees it as 2–3 years
- Leadership can articulate what the industry will look like but not what the firm specifically needs to do
- The firm has a vision statement everyone endorses but no one can translate into specific strategic choices

At the organisational level:
- Different business units are pursuing different, sometimes contradictory, transformation agendas
- Employees cannot explain how their work connects to the transformation strategy
- Decisions are made locally without reference to an overarching transformation direction

Common root causes:
- Dominant Logic Inertia: Leaders' past success reinforces mental models that don't apply to the disrupted landscape
- Threat Minimisation: Psychological defence mechanisms lead leaders to underestimate disruption
- Complexity Avoidance: The implications of true transformation are so uncomfortable that leaders avoid confronting them fully
- Echo Chambers: Leaders hear primarily from people who share their existing views

### How CGS Addresses Clarity Gaps

**External Sensing Workshops**: Facilitated sessions analysing external disruption signals and building a shared view of where the industry is heading.

**Scenario Planning**: Development of 3–4 concrete future scenarios for the industry, stress-tested against real data.

**Competitor Deep Dives**: Rigorous analysis of what specific competitors are actually doing — not their press releases but their operational reality.

**The Disruption Snapshot**: CGS's signature deliverable for building Clarity — a single, concise document capturing where the industry is on the disruption continuum, where the client sits relative to peers, what the window for action is, and what specific strategic choices the client must make.

### Clarity Severity Levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Low | Leadership broadly aligned. Minor variations in detail. Maintenance, not crisis management. |
| Medium | Meaningful gaps in leadership alignment — different views on timing, priority, or required change. A structured alignment process can close these gaps. |
| High | Leadership fundamentally divided on the nature or urgency of the disruption. Without resolving this, any transformation investment is at risk. |
| Critical | The CEO or board operating from a fundamentally incorrect mental model. Cannot be closed without confronting at the most senior level. Full Dominant Logic Inertia. |

## CAPABILITY

### Definition
Capability is the degree to which the organisation possesses — or is systematically building — the skills, processes, technology, data, and culture required to execute its transformation strategy.

CGS assesses Capability across five dimensions:
1. **People and Talent**: Right people, right skills, right mindset
2. **Processes and Operating Model**: Processes designed for the new business model, not just the old one with AI tools layered on
3. **Technology and Data**: Infrastructure, data architecture, and AI capabilities required
4. **Culture and Mindset**: Supports experimentation, learning, speed, and external orientation
5. **Governance and Leadership Capability**: Governance structures and leadership skills matching transformation demands

### What a Capability Gap Looks Like

People gaps:
- Strategy requires data scientists, AI engineers, or product managers that don't exist or are understaffed
- Key talent departing for competitors offering more advanced AI environments
- Existing employees resist new tools because they haven't been trained or supported

Process gaps:
- AI tools deployed on top of processes designed for human-only execution (bolt-on innovation)
- No clear protocols for human-AI collaboration in core workflows
- Data flows between systems are fragmented, manual, or unreliable

Technology gaps:
- Data siloed in legacy systems with no unified data platform
- AI deployments are isolated use cases without a coherent architecture
- Security and compliance frameworks for AI not developed

Culture gaps:
- Failure is punished, so no one experiments
- Success defined by defending existing business, not building new capabilities
- Deeply entrenched organisational silos prevent cross-functional collaboration

### How CGS Addresses Capability Gaps

**Capability Assessment**: Structured diagnostic mapping current capabilities against transformation strategy requirements. Produces a detailed gap map — what is missing, where, and how critical.

**Build vs Buy vs Partner Analysis**:
- Build: Develop internally through hiring, training, process redesign. Slowest but most durable competitive advantage.
- Buy: Acquire a company or team that already has the capability. Fastest but most expensive and carries integration risk.
- Partner: Access through strategic partnership, joint venture, or outsourcing. Middle path for capabilities that are important but not differentiating.

**CGS Transformation Labs**: Hands-on working sessions in which CGS works alongside client teams to prototype new processes, test new tools, and build organisational muscle memory for new ways of working.

### Capability Gap Severity Levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Low | Organisation has most required capabilities. Targeted investments can close gaps within current planning cycle. |
| Medium | Meaningful capability gaps in 1–2 dimensions. Closing them requires deliberate investment and 12–18 months. |
| High | Significant gaps across multiple dimensions. Major investment, structural changes, and 18–36 months of sustained effort required. |
| Critical | Lacks foundational capabilities to execute even the first stage of the transformation strategy. Emergency intervention required. |

## CONVICTION

### Definition
Conviction is the degree to which the organisation's leadership has the courage, alignment, and sustained commitment to drive the transformation to completion.

Of the Three Cs, Conviction is the hardest to build and the easiest to lose. Transformation requires making decisions with incomplete information, accepting short-term costs for long-term benefit, managing significant organisational resistance, and maintaining direction when early results are mixed.

Conviction is not the same as enthusiasm. CGS has observed a consistent pattern — The Conviction Curve: Conviction peaks at the start of an engagement, dips significantly at 6–12 months when costs are mounting and resistance is high, and only recovers if leaders actively maintain it.

### What a Conviction Gap Looks Like

At the individual leader level:
- A leader publicly endorses the transformation but privately hedges
- Investment decisions scaled back at the first sign of short-term financial pressure
- Leaders avoid the difficult conversations transformation requires
- Leaders' calendars don't reflect the priority they claim the transformation has
- Transformation framed in ways that minimise its implications

At the leadership team level:
- Nominal consensus on strategy but active resistance beneath the surface
- Decisions made in steering committees relitigated at every subsequent meeting
- Some leaders blocking progress to protect their turf
- The team cannot present a unified front to the organisation

At the organisational level:
- Middle management actively resists because they correctly perceive it threatens their roles
- Budget allocations don't reflect the stated transformation priority
- Transformation initiative is the first thing cut when quarterly targets are at risk

### The Most Common Conviction Destroyers

**Short-termism**: Quarterly earnings pressure. Board impatience. The tyranny of short-term metrics over long-term transformation.

**The First Failure**: The first significant setback — a failed pilot, a key hire who doesn't work out — often becomes a turning point. Leaders with genuine Conviction use setbacks as learning opportunities. Leaders with fragile Conviction use them as evidence the transformation isn't working.

**Stakeholder Fatigue**: After 12–18 months of continuous change, even committed leaders begin to tire. Without active renewal of energy and momentum, Conviction erodes.

**Social Pressure**: In most established organisations, culture rewards conformity and punishes deviance. Leaders who champion transformation swim against this current.

### How CGS Addresses Conviction Gaps

**Honest Diagnosis**: CGS does not allow Conviction gaps to remain unspoken. If a leader's behaviour signals fragile Conviction, CGS names it directly. This is part of CGS's "respectfully tells the truth" value.

**The Investment Case**: Building and maintaining a compelling, data-grounded investment case that connects transformation investments to competitive outcomes in terms the CFO and board find compelling.

**Quick Wins Architecture**: Deliberately designing the transformation roadmap to produce visible, credible early wins that build organisational belief in the transformation's viability.

**Leadership Alignment Rituals**: Regular, structured forums in which the leadership team actively renews its commitment — reviews progress, addresses doubts, resolves disagreements, and recommits to the plan.

**Executive Coaching**: In some engagements, CGS provides direct coaching to individual leaders on the personal dimensions of Conviction.

### Conviction Severity Levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Low | Leadership broadly committed. Individual waverers exist but don't represent systemic risk. |
| Medium | Meaningful Conviction gaps at the leadership team level. Significant effort required to maintain alignment and prevent backsliding. |
| High | Key leaders — potentially the CEO or critical C-suite — have fragile or declining Conviction. Transformation is at risk of being scoped down or abandoned. Urgent intervention required. |
| Critical | Board or CEO has withdrawn genuine Conviction. Without a fundamental reset, the transformation cannot succeed. |

## The Interplay Between the Three Cs

**Clarity enables Capability investment**: Without shared Clarity, it is impossible to make confident decisions about which capabilities to build. Organisations with low Clarity make scattered, reactive capability investments that don't accumulate into a coherent competitive position.

**Capability builds Conviction**: Early evidence of capability progress — a successful pilot, a new hire who demonstrates what's possible — is one of the most powerful ways to sustain Conviction.

**Conviction enables Clarity maintenance**: Maintaining organisational Clarity requires leaders willing to have difficult conversations and sustain the message over time. Without Conviction, Clarity erodes.

**The Death Spiral**: When all three Cs are low, organisations enter a self-reinforcing decline. Low Clarity → unfocused Capability investment → few visible wins → eroding Conviction → reduced willingness to confront Clarity questions.

**The Virtuous Cycle**: When all three Cs are high and reinforcing each other, transformation accelerates. Clear direction → focused capability investment → growing capability → wins that sustain Conviction → strong Conviction maintains Clarity.

## Application to CGS Momentum — How Aria Uses the Three Cs

**For Project Memory Q&A**: Aria references the specific Three Cs findings from the client's engagement. Example: "In your CGS assessment, your Clarity Gap was rated High — specifically, there was a significant disconnect between your CEO's view of the transformation timeline (5–7 years) and CGS's assessment (2–3 years). Your Capability Gap was rated Critical, particularly in the People dimension where you had no Chief Digital Officer and limited internal data engineering capability."

**For Health Check-in**: Each monthly check-in includes questions designed to track progress against the Three Cs gaps identified at engagement close. Aria tracks whether Clarity is being maintained, whether Capability gaps are being closed on schedule, and whether Conviction signals are positive or concerning.

**Escalation trigger**: A significant deterioration in any of the Three Cs — particularly Conviction — is one of the clearest signals that a client may need a human CGS consultant. Aria is trained to detect these signals and escalate appropriately.
