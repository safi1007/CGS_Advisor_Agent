# CGS Inertia Removal Playbook

---

## Overview

Inertia is the single most consistent reason transformation initiatives fail.
Not lack of strategy. Not lack of technology. Not lack of budget.
Inertia.

CGS defines inertia as the forces — mental, structural, and cultural — that
cause organisations to resist necessary change even when their leaders
intellectually understand that change is required. It is the gap between
knowing and doing. Between deciding and acting. Between launching and
sustaining.

CGS has identified two distinct types of inertia that operate at different
levels of the organisation and require fundamentally different removal
tactics. Both must be addressed. Removing one while ignoring the other
produces temporary progress at best.

The two types are:
- **Dominant Logic Inertia** — operates at the Executive Level
- **Structural Inertia** — operates at the Firm Level

---

## TYPE 1: DOMINANT LOGIC INERTIA
*The Executive Level*

### Definition

Dominant Logic Inertia is the resistance to transformation that originates
in the mental models of senior leaders. It is not ignorance — it is the
accumulated wisdom of successful careers that has calcified into
assumptions so deeply held that leaders no longer recognise them as
assumptions. They experience them as facts.

The term "Dominant Logic" was coined by management scholars Prahalad and
Bettis to describe the lens through which senior executives interpret their
business environment. In stable industries, a strong Dominant Logic is an
asset — it allows fast, confident decisions based on deep pattern
recognition. In disrupting industries, it becomes a liability — it causes
leaders to misread, minimise, or dismiss signals that don't fit the
existing model.

CGS founder Gregg Garrett has observed in over 15 years of transformation
advisory work: "The most dangerous thing a leader can say is 'I've seen
this before.' Because the disruption they're looking at almost certainly
has elements they have never seen before — and their confidence that they
understand it prevents them from asking the questions that would reveal
what they're missing."

### Root Causes

**Past success**: Leaders who have built successful careers by executing a
particular model find it genuinely difficult to believe that model is now
wrong. Their success is evidence that their mental model works — which
makes it psychologically costly to abandon.

**Pattern matching to incomplete analogies**: Leaders often compare current
disruption to past disruptions they survived. "We've been through changes
before and we always adapted." The comparison feels reassuring but is
often misleading — today's disruption frequently has structural
characteristics (speed, comprehensiveness, network effects) that make it
categorically different from past changes.

**Threat minimisation**: There is a natural psychological tendency to
underestimate threats that are uncomfortable to confront. When fully
acknowledging the disruption implies massive investment, significant
organisational pain, and personal risk, the unconscious mind finds reasons
to believe the threat is smaller or more distant than the data suggests.

**Selective information processing**: Leaders pay more attention to data
that confirms their existing view and discount data that challenges it.
They spend time with advisors who share their perspective. They interpret
ambiguous signals in the direction of their preference.

**Social reinforcement**: In most organisations, it is career-limiting to
tell the CEO they are wrong. So the CEO's mental model gets reinforced
constantly by a team that has learned not to challenge it.

### How to Recognise Dominant Logic Inertia

**Language signals** — listen for these phrases in leadership conversations:
- "Our customers are different / more sophisticated / more loyal"
- "We've been through disruption before and we always found a way"
- "The technology isn't mature enough yet for our industry"
- "Our competitive moat is too strong to be disrupted quickly"
- "The analysts are overhyping this — the real timeline is much longer"
- "We need to wait until there's a clear winner before we commit"
- "We tried something like this five years ago and it didn't work"

**Behavioural signals:**
- Leaders dismiss disruption signals as industry noise rather than engaging
  with them seriously
- Strategy sessions focus on optimising the existing model rather than
  questioning it
- Transformation investments are consistently scaled back or delayed
- The CEO's external speeches contradict the internal urgency of the
  transformation
- Disconfirming data is explained away rather than investigated

**Organisational signals:**
- The firm's strategic plan reads like a continuation of the current
  business model with AI tools added
- The transformation initiative is led by a mid-level executive rather
  than the CEO
- There is no burning platform narrative — no clear articulation of why
  the status quo is unsustainable
- Key transformation decisions require unanimous consensus rather than
  courageous individual leadership

### CGS Tactics for Removing Dominant Logic Inertia

**Tactic 1 — The Burning Platform**
Create an undeniable, visceral case for why the status quo is unsustainable.
Not an abstract argument about industry trends but a specific, concrete
demonstration of existential risk. CGS uses competitor case studies, client
defection data, talent market signals, and financial model projections to
make the threat real and personal.

The goal is not to create panic — it is to create a shared, honest
acknowledgment among leadership that the current trajectory is not viable.
Once leaders genuinely believe the burning platform is real, Dominant Logic
Inertia begins to dissolve.

**Tactic 2 — Disconfirming Evidence Workshops**
Facilitated sessions specifically designed to expose leadership to evidence
that challenges their current mental model. CGS brings in customers who
have defected, competitors who have succeeded, and market data that
contradicts comfortable assumptions.

The key is creating psychological safety to have the disconfirming
conversation. Most leadership teams have never had a genuine discussion
about what would have to be true for their current strategy to be wrong.

**Tactic 3 — External Reference Visits**
Taking leadership teams to visit organisations in other industries that have
successfully navigated similar disruptions. Seeing a transformed firm in
person — talking to its leaders, understanding what they gave up and what
they gained — is consistently more powerful than any amount of data or
analysis.

**Tactic 4 — The Pre-Mortem**
A structured exercise in which the leadership team imagines they are five
years in the future and the transformation has failed. They then work
backwards to identify all the reasons it failed. This technique bypasses
the psychological defences that prevent leaders from honestly assessing
current risks.

**Tactic 5 — Reframing the Identity**
Dominant Logic Inertia is often tied to organisational identity — "we are
a manufacturing company" or "we are a relationship-based services firm."
CGS helps leaders reframe their identity in terms of the outcomes they
create for customers rather than the methods they currently use to create
them. This creates psychological permission to change the methods while
preserving the core purpose.

**Tactic 6 — Direct CEO Coaching**
When Dominant Logic Inertia is concentrated in a single powerful leader,
CGS may engage that leader directly in executive coaching. This is the most
sensitive and most high-impact intervention. CGS's credibility, its
track record of having "sat in the seat," and its willingness to
respectfully tell difficult truths makes this kind of direct intervention
possible.

## TYPE 2: STRUCTURAL INERTIA
*The Firm Level*

### Definition

Structural Inertia is the resistance to transformation that is embedded in
the organisation's systems, processes, incentives, governance structures,
culture, and talent profile. Unlike Dominant Logic Inertia — which lives
in leaders' minds — Structural Inertia lives in the organisation itself.

An organisation can have leaders who fully understand and embrace the
need to transform, and still be blocked by Structural Inertia. The systems
and processes that made the organisation successful in the past are
optimised for the past. Changing them is slow, expensive, and painful —
and the organisation resists even when leaders want it to change.

CGS describes Structural Inertia as "the organisation's immune system."
When something new and different is introduced, the organisation's natural
response is to neutralise, assimilate, or expel it — not out of malice
but because the existing system is optimised for stability and the new
thing disrupts that stability.

### Root Causes

**Misaligned incentives**: If the incentive system rewards defending
existing business, people will defend existing business — even if their
leaders tell them to do something different. Incentives are the most
powerful determinant of behaviour, and they almost always lag behind
strategic intent.

**Legacy processes**: Core business processes have been optimised over
years or decades for the existing business model. Redesigning them is
expensive, risky, and requires capabilities the organisation may not have.
So the organisation deploys new tools on top of old processes and wonders
why transformation is so slow.

**Governance structures**: Many large organisations have governance
structures designed for a world of slow, predictable change — elaborate
approval hierarchies, lengthy budget cycles, consensus-based
decision-making. These structures are antithetical to the speed and
experimentation that transformation requires.

**Talent profile mismatch**: The organisation has spent years hiring,
developing, and promoting people who are excellent at executing the
existing model. These people have deeply invested careers in the current
way of doing things, and they are often not the people who will thrive
in the transformed organisation.

**Culture of conformity**: Most established organisations have cultures
that reward fitting in, executing reliably, and avoiding failure. These
cultures are deeply inhospitable to the experimentation, risk-taking, and
learning from failure that transformation requires.

**Organisational silos**: Functions that have operated independently for
years — IT, Operations, Sales, Marketing, Finance — struggle to
collaborate in the cross-functional ways that transformation often demands.
Silos protect resources, status, and power; they don't give these up easily.

### How to Recognise Structural Inertia

**Process signals:**
- Every new initiative requires approval from a committee that meets
  quarterly
- It takes more than 6 months to hire a key transformation role
- Technology procurement processes designed for legacy ERP systems are
  being applied to AI tools
- The transformation pilot was a success but scaling it requires a budget
  cycle that doesn't open for another 8 months

**Incentive signals:**
- Sales teams are paid on revenue from existing products, not new ones
- Leaders are measured on quarterly EBITDA, not on transformation milestones
- The performance review system does not include any assessment of
  transformation contribution
- The last person who took a risk and failed was quietly managed out

**Talent signals:**
- The transformation requires capabilities that don't exist in the
  organisation and are taking 6–12 months to hire
- Key transformation leaders are being poached by more agile competitors
- The internal talent pipeline has been optimised for the old model and
  has no one ready for the new roles
- Training and development investment is concentrated on existing skills,
  not future ones

**Cultural signals:**
- "That's not how we do things here" is an accepted reason to reject a
  new idea
- The transformation team is viewed with suspicion or resentment by the
  rest of the organisation
- Executives who were associated with past failed initiatives are reluctant
  to champion new ones
- Innovation and experimentation are celebrated in speeches but not
  in resource allocation

### CGS Tactics for Removing Structural Inertia

**Tactic 1 — Incentive Realignment**
Change what you measure and reward before you expect behaviour to change.
CGS works with HR and Finance to design incentive structures that reward
transformation behaviours — innovation, cross-functional collaboration,
learning from failure, capability building — alongside or instead of
purely financial metrics.

This is one of the most impactful and most difficult inertia removal
tactics. Incentive system changes touch everyone's pay and career
prospects, and they generate significant resistance. But without them,
all other tactics are fighting the current.

**Tactic 2 — Process Redesign**
Identify the 3–5 core processes that are most critical to the
transformation and redesign them from scratch around the optimal
human-AI configuration, rather than layering new tools on old processes.

CGS uses transformation labs — intensive, hands-on design sprints in
which cross-functional teams redesign core processes together. The lab
format is important because it creates shared ownership of the new
processes, which dramatically improves adoption.

**Tactic 3 — Governance Simplification**
Strip back the governance structures that are slowing transformation
decisions. Create fast-track approval pathways for transformation
investments. Give transformation leaders budget authority that doesn't
require consensus. Establish a transformation steering committee that
meets weekly, not quarterly.

**Tactic 4 — Strategic Talent Moves**
Make highly visible changes to the talent profile at the leadership level.
Hire a Chief Digital Officer or Chief AI Officer. Promote leaders who have
demonstrated transformation behaviours. Exit leaders who are actively
blocking the transformation.

These moves send powerful signals to the organisation about what is
valued and what is required. They are more effective than any amount
of communication about the importance of transformation.

**Tactic 5 — Protected Space**
Create protected spaces within the organisation — innovation labs, skunkworks
teams, transformation hubs — where the normal rules don't apply. These
spaces operate with different processes, different incentives, and
different culture. They give the new model room to breathe and prove
itself before it has to compete with the existing model on the existing
model's terms.

**Tactic 6 — Culture Shift Programme**
Culture change is the slowest and most difficult form of inertia removal.
It requires sustained, consistent action across multiple levers over
2–3 years minimum. CGS's culture shift programme focuses on:
- Visible leadership behaviour modelling new cultural norms
- Story-telling that celebrates transformation behaviours and outcomes
- Systematic removal of processes and structures that reinforce the old culture
- Recognition and reward systems that make new cultural behaviours visible
  and valuable
- Hiring and promotion decisions that select for cultural fit with the
  new model, not the old one

**Tactic 7 — Middle Management Activation**
Middle management is simultaneously the most important and most overlooked
audience in transformation. They are the translation layer between strategy
and execution. They control the day-to-day environment in which front-line
employees work. And they often have the most to lose from transformation.

CGS works specifically with middle management to address their concerns
honestly, involve them in transformation design, give them new skills and
roles in the transformed organisation, and convert them from passive
resistors to active champions.

## Combined Inertia Removal: The Sequencing Question

CGS recommends addressing both types of inertia simultaneously, but the
sequencing of specific tactics matters.

**Start with Dominant Logic Inertia** because Structural Inertia removal
requires leadership commitment and resource investment. If leaders are not
genuinely convinced of the need to transform, they will not make the
difficult governance, incentive, and talent decisions that Structural
Inertia removal requires. Addressing Dominant Logic Inertia first creates
the leadership Conviction that makes Structural Inertia removal possible.

**Then attack the highest-leverage Structural Inertia** — typically
incentives and talent, in that order. These have the broadest
organisational impact and send the clearest signals that the transformation
is real.

**Sustain both in parallel** for the duration of the transformation. Inertia
does not stay removed — it reasserts itself under pressure, when leaders
change, or when the organisation faces setbacks. Inertia removal is not a
project with a completion date; it is an ongoing leadership discipline.

## Inertia Removal and Aria

When a client asks Aria why their organisation is resisting change, Aria
should diagnose the type of inertia based on the client's description and
reference the relevant tactics from this playbook.

**Key diagnostic questions Aria uses:**
- "Is the resistance coming from senior leaders who don't believe the
  transformation is necessary or urgent?" → Dominant Logic Inertia
- "Is the resistance coming from middle management or front-line employees
  who feel threatened or unsupported?" → Structural Inertia
- "Are the right people bought in but the organisation's systems and
  processes making progress slow and difficult?" → Structural Inertia
- "Are leaders saying the right things but not making the investment
  decisions or difficult choices the transformation requires?" → Conviction
  gap combined with Dominant Logic Inertia

**Escalation signal**: If a client's description suggests that a key
executive (particularly the CEO, CFO, or board) has fallen into Dominant
Logic Inertia and is actively blocking transformation, Aria should escalate
to a human CGS consultant. This is too sensitive and too high-stakes for
agent-only resolution.
