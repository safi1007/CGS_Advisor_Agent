# CGS Organizational Intelligence Maturity Model


---

## Overview

The CGS Organizational Intelligence Maturity Model describes the five levels through which organisations progress as they move from treating AI as a tool to embedding intelligence as a core organisational capability. The model is built on a fundamental insight from CGS's research: **the value of AI is not determined by the sophistication of the technology, but by the maturity of the organisation using it.**

Organisations at Level A1 and A2 consistently underperform the value their AI investments should generate, not because their technology is wrong, but because their mindset, people, processes, and governance structures have not evolved to match. Conversely, organisations that reach Level A4 and A5 achieve compounding returns from their intelligence investments.

The model is used by CGS in two ways:
1. **Diagnostic**: To assess where a client organisation currently sits across four dimensions
2. **Roadmap**: To define the specific changes required to progress to the next level

---

## The Four Scoring Dimensions

Every organisation is scored across four dimensions. An organisation's overall maturity level is determined by the lowest-scoring dimension — you are only as mature as your weakest link.

### Dimension 1: Strategy
How does intelligence factor into the organisation's strategic planning and competitive positioning? This dimension assesses whether the organisation has an Intelligence Strategy — a deliberate, board-level commitment to defining the optimal configuration of human and machine intelligence — as distinct from a tactical AI roadmap or list of AI tools.

Signs of low Strategy maturity: AI initiatives are driven by IT, not the C-suite. No clear articulation of where AI will create competitive advantage. AI investments justified by cost reduction rather than capability creation. No AI governance framework.

Signs of high Strategy maturity: The board reviews intelligence strategy quarterly. AI capability is a core input to strategic planning. Intelligence density is tracked as a business metric.

### Dimension 2: People
How are the organisation's people equipped, motivated, and organised to work with AI? This is consistently the hardest dimension to advance and the most commonly underestimated. Technology can be deployed in weeks. People change in months and years.

Signs of low People maturity: Employees view AI as a threat. No AI literacy programme. Only a small number of specialists understand how to work with AI. High resistance to AI adoption.

Signs of high People maturity: All employees have baseline AI literacy. Leaders model AI-augmented working. New roles exist (AI Product Owners, Intelligence Stewards). People view AI as amplifying their capabilities.

### Dimension 3: Process
How have the organisation's core processes been redesigned to incorporate intelligence? The most common failure mode in enterprise AI adoption is deploying AI on top of processes designed for human-only execution — producing bolt-on innovation rather than transformative capability.

Signs of low Process maturity: AI tools added to existing workflows without redesigning them. No standard protocols for human-AI handoffs. Data flows are fragmented.

Signs of high Process maturity: Core processes fundamentally redesigned around optimal human-AI configuration. Clear protocols for what decisions are made by humans vs AI. Learning loops embedded — AI outputs continuously evaluated and used to improve.

### Dimension 4: Technology
How mature is the organisation's AI technology stack, data infrastructure, and governance? This is typically the easiest dimension to advance but the most commonly over-invested in relative to People and Process.

Signs of low Technology maturity: Data siloed across systems. No AI governance framework. AI models deployed without monitoring. Security and compliance frameworks for AI not developed.

Signs of high Technology maturity: Unified data platform. AI governance frameworks cover model selection, deployment, monitoring, and retirement. Explainability and auditability standards exist. Multi-vendor flexibility.

## The Five Maturity Levels

### Level A1 — REPLACEMENT
*"AI replaces humans for defined tasks"*

**Intelligence Mindset**: Intelligence is finite and competitive. AI is a tool that does some human tasks more cheaply.

**View of Humans and AI**: Substitutes. Zero-sum.

**Typical AI Use**: Task automation, data entry, document processing, basic prediction, RPA.

**Human Role**: Executors and monitors. Humans oversee AI-automated tasks and handle exceptions.

**Organisational Symbols**: AI initiatives justified by cost savings. High fear and resistance. Minimal reskilling. AI owned by IT. Success measured by headcount reduction.

**Primary Outcome**: Local efficiency gains. No increase in Organisational IQ. Often creates resentment that impedes future progress.

**In practice**: A manufacturing company deploys RPA to automate invoice processing, saving 3 FTEs. The technology works but employees are anxious, and there is no strategic vision for what comes next.

**CGS Coaching**: A1 is a legitimate starting point but organisations that get stuck here fail to realise compounding returns. The key shift is from "what tasks can AI replace?" to "how can AI augment our people's capabilities?"

---

### Level A2 — AUGMENTATION
*"AI supports human decision-making"*

**Intelligence Mindset**: Intelligence improves through better tools. AI makes humans more effective at existing jobs.

**View of Humans and AI**: AI supports humans but doesn't think with them. Humans remain the primary decision-makers.

**Typical AI Use**: Dashboards and analytics, AI-generated recommendations, co-pilots (GitHub Copilot, AI writing assistants), forecasting, customer segmentation.

**Human Role**: Decision-makers using AI outputs. Humans interpret recommendations and make final decisions.

**Organisational Symbols**: Tool-focused AI training. AI owned by IT and Analytics. Siloed use cases. Success measured by productivity improvements in specific workflows.

**Primary Outcome**: Faster execution of existing processes. Individual productivity gains. Limited strategic impact — the organisation is doing the same things faster, not fundamentally different things.

**In practice**: A professional services firm deploys AI writing tools. Consultants produce first drafts 40% faster. But the firm's service model and competitive positioning are unchanged.

**CGS Coaching**: Most organisations plateau at A2 because it feels productive. The transition to A3 requires moving from "AI as my assistant" to "AI and I as co-investigators." This demands a change in how people are trained — not just using AI outputs, but engaging with AI in genuine dialogue.

**Important note**: Most CGS clients arrive at A2. It is the most common starting point for mid-large enterprises as of 2025.

---

### Level A3 — DISTRIBUTED
*"Humans and AI co-produce insight"*

**Intelligence Mindset**: Intelligence emerges from the interaction between humans and AI. Neither alone is as capable as both together.

**View of Humans and AI**: Co-producers of insight. Humans and AI engage in genuine dialogue to discover patterns and generate understanding.

**Typical AI Use**: Pattern discovery, scenario generation, AI as thinking partner, knowledge synthesis across the organisation.

**Human Role**: Interpreters and sense-makers. Humans provide context, judgment, and meaning to AI-generated patterns.

**Organisational Symbols**: Shared data platforms enabling cross-functional AI use. Emerging governance frameworks. AI literacy beginning to broaden. Cross-functional AI task forces.

**Primary Outcome**: Higher-quality insights than either humans or AI would produce alone. Beginning of learning loops.

**In practice**: A retailer's strategy team uses AI to analyse millions of customer transactions, identify behaviour patterns, and generate scenario forecasts. The human team interprets these through their knowledge of customer psychology, producing insights neither could reach alone.

**CGS Coaching**: A3 is where transformation begins to feel real. The key challenge is scale — A3 capabilities often exist in pockets but haven't spread across the organisation.

---

### Level A4 — COLLABORATIVE
*"AI and humans as complementary collaborators"*

**Intelligence Mindset**: Intelligence is generative and expandable. Human and machine intelligence are fundamentally complementary.

**View of Humans and AI**: Complementary collaborators. Each designed for different tasks; together vastly more capable than either alone.

**Typical AI Use**: Hypothesis generation, complex multi-step reasoning with human oversight, agentic AI handling extended workflows, AI-human collaborative strategy development.

**Human Role**: Judgment, creativity, and goal-setting. Humans define what matters, set ethical boundaries, and make decisions requiring wisdom and accountability.

**Organisational Symbols**: Clear AI operating model across the organisation. Strong governance with AI ethics committee. Strategic reskilling redefining all roles around human-AI collaboration. AI performance metrics embedded in business KPIs. Senior AI leadership in C-suite.

**Primary Outcome**: Faster, better decisions that compound over time. The organisation learns continuously. Becomes measurably more intelligent over time.

**In practice**: A consulting firm at A4 has redefined every role around the Human + Agent model. Junior analysts work with AI agents for data gathering, framework application, and draft production. Senior consultants focus on client relationships, strategic judgment, and synthesis.

**CGS Coaching**: Getting to A4 requires confronting the most difficult human question: "What is distinctively human about the work we do, and how do we protect and amplify that?"

**A4 is the target level for most CGS client engagements over an 18–24 month horizon.**

---

### Level A5 — MISSION-DRIVEN
*"Intelligence is the organisation's core operating system"*

**Intelligence Mindset**: Intelligence is collective and purpose-driven. The entire operating model is designed around optimal human-machine intelligence deployment.

**View of Humans and AI**: Integrated cognitive system. The boundary between human and machine intelligence is fluid.

**Typical AI Use**: Continuous sensing and foresight. AI agents handle entire business functions. The organisation's intelligence compounds — it gets measurably smarter every quarter.

**Human Role**: Stewards of mission and ethics. Humans define purpose, set values, make decisions requiring humanity, and ensure intelligence is directed toward meaningful ends.

**Organisational Symbols**: AI as core capability tracked at board level. Intelligence metrics tied to executive compensation. Recognised as a leader in human-AI collaboration. AI ethics embedded in culture, not just policy.

**Primary Outcome**: Sustained competitive advantage and adaptability. The organisation can respond to disruption faster than competitors can react.

**Note**: As of 2026, very few organisations have reached A5. A5 is directionally important — it tells organisations what they are building toward — but A4 is the realistic near-term ambition for most CGS clients.

## The Intelligence Value Formula

**V = ∫(H_strategy · M_execution)^Id dt**

Where:
- **V** = Value generated over time
- **H_strategy** = Quality of human strategic judgment
- **M_execution** = Scale and consistency of machine execution
- **Id** = Intelligence Density (degree to which intelligence is embedded throughout the organisation)
- **t** = Time

Intelligence Density acts as an exponent — small increases in how broadly intelligence is embedded produce large, compounding increases in value over time.

## The Minimum Viable Human Organisation (MVHO)

A concept developed by CGS Advisors to help leaders think about the endpoint of intelligent transformation. The MVHO asks: "What is the minimum human organisation needed to maximally leverage machine intelligence?"

For most professional services firms, the MVHO centres on:
- Executive judgment and decision-making under uncertainty
- Client relationships built on trust, empathy, and shared context
- Creative and strategic thinking requiring integration of values with analysis
- Ethical accountability and responsibility for outcomes

Everything else — knowledge retrieval, framework application, progress tracking, document generation, pattern matching — is a candidate for intelligence augmentation or full AI ownership.

Aria's role in CGS Momentum is explicitly the MVHO in action: Aria handles knowledge, frameworks, tracking, and document generation. CGS consultants focus exclusively on judgment, relationships, and strategy that only humans can provide.
